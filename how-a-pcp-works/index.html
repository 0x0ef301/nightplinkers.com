Section 01

What Is a PCP Airgun?

PCP stands for Pre-Charged Pneumatic. Instead of a spring or gas ram you cock before each shot, a PCP stores a reservoir of high-pressure air — compressed to anywhere from 2,000 to 4,350 PSI (roughly 140–300 bar) — that's loaded before you shoot. Each pull of the trigger releases a precise, metered burst of that stored air to launch the pellet.

The result: near-zero recoil, extreme consistency shot-to-shot, and the ability to fire multiple shots from a single charge. PCPs dominate competition shooting and are the go-to platform for hunters and serious plinkers.

2,900–4,350
Typical fill pressure (PSI)
That's 2,900–4,350 PSI (200–300 bar) — roughly 20× a car tire. The reservoir is engineered to handle it safely.
20–100+
Shots per fill
Depends on calibre, tune, and whether your rifle is regulated. Many .22 PCPs give 40–60 usable shots.
~0
Felt recoil
No piston slamming forward and back. PCPs have a tiny rearward pulse — barely noticeable. Scopes love them.
±1–3
FPS spread (regulated)
A well-regulated PCP fires every shot at virtually the same velocity. Competition-level consistency, every time.
New to PCPs? The main thing to grasp: you're not compressing air when you shoot — you've already done that during the fill. The gun is just managing how much of that stored air escapes per shot.

```
Section 04

Pressure & Safety

PCPs operate at pressures that demand respect. Understanding the zones on your gauge isn't optional — it's the first thing you learn.

```
◂ DRAG TO EXPLORE ▸
0–1,450 PSI — Low / Spent
Gun still fires but velocity is dropping. Accuracy deteriorates. Time to refill before your shot string falls off the power curve.
1,450–3,335 PSI — Optimal Range
Most PCPs shoot their best in this band (approx. 1,450–3,335 PSI / 100–230 bar). Regulated guns maintain consistent velocity throughout. This is your working pressure.
3,335–3,625 PSI — Near Max Fill
Approaching rated fill for many rifles (approx. 3,335–3,625 PSI / 230–250 bar). Stop here unless your rifle is rated higher. Check your manual.
3,625+ PSI — Danger Zone
Overfill territory for most rifles (above ~3,625 PSI / 250 bar). Can blow seals, damage the valve, or — in extreme cases — cause catastrophic failure. Never exceed the rated fill pressure.
```
Always check your rifle's rated fill pressure before connecting a fill source. It's printed on the reservoir or in the manual. A 2,900 PSI (200 bar) rifle does not become safer if you "just fill it to 3,335 PSI (230 bar)."
Never fill in an enclosed space. A catastrophic failure releases enormous energy. Fill outdoors, pointed away from yourself and others, with the barrel pointed in a safe direction.
Section 05

The Regulator

A regulator is a pressure-reducing valve between the reservoir and the valve/hammer. It steps down high reservoir pressure to a fixed, lower setpoint — delivering the same working pressure to the valve on every single shot, regardless of how full the reservoir is.

Regulated guns bleed reservoir pressure down through the regulator to a fixed setpoint — say, 1,600 PSI (110 bar) — before it ever reaches the valve. Whether the reservoir reads 3,335 PSI (230 bar) or 1,885 PSI (130 bar), the valve sees the same pressure every shot. Result: tiny velocity spreads, predictable shot counts, easy to tune.
Unregulated guns feed reservoir pressure directly to the valve. At high fill, too much air per shot = lower velocity (overpowered, valve bounces closed). As pressure drops it enters the sweet spot — peak velocity. Then it falls off as pressure depletes. You tune for the sweet spot and work within it.
Section 06

The Fill Cycle

There are three ways to charge a PCP. Each has its own workflow, pace, and practical trade-offs. Select a method to walk through it step by step.

Dive Bottle Fill Step 1 / 5
Step 01 / 05
Check the bottle pressure
Read the valve gauge on your dive cylinder. A full 88 cu ft (12L) bottle at 4,350 PSI (300 bar) gives you many fills. If pressure is below your rifle's rated fill, the bottle can't top the rifle up — arrange a refill at a dive shop or HPA compressor service first.
0RIFLE4,350 PSI
Step 02 / 05
Attach the fill hose
Thread the DIN or yoke connector onto the cylinder valve hand-tight. Connect the other end's Foster quick-connect to your rifle's fill port. Check for a bleed screw on the hose — it should be closed.
0RIFLE PRESSURE4,350 PSI
Step 03 / 05
Open the bottle valve — slowly
Turn the cylinder valve slowly — quarter turn, pause, listen. Air pressure equalises between the bottle and rifle reservoir. Watch the rifle's manometer (if fitted) or the gauge on the fill hose rise steadily toward your target fill pressure.
0↑ FILLING4,350 PSI
Step 04 / 05
Close the valve at target pressure
When the gauge reaches your rifle's rated fill pressure (e.g. 2,900 PSI / 200 bar, or 3,335 PSI / 230 bar — check your manual), close the cylinder valve. Do not overfill. The pressure may creep slightly after you close it — that's normal equalisation, not a problem.
0TARGET ✓4,350 PSI
Step 05 / 05
Bleed, disconnect, shoot
Open the bleed screw on the fill hose to vent residual pressure in the line. Once pressure is relieved, disconnect the Foster fitting from the fill port. The rifle is now at full charge. Store the cylinder valve closed. You're ready to shoot.
0CHARGED ✓4,350 PSI
Dive bottle pros: Fast fills (seconds), high capacity (dozens of fills per cylinder), quiet. Con: Bottles need periodic hydro testing and a dive shop or HPA compressor to refill.
Electric Compressor Fill Step 1 / 5
Step 01 / 05
Prepare the compressor
Check oil level (if oil-lubed) or that filters are seated (oil-free). Ensure the output pressure setting matches or exceeds your rifle's fill pressure. Connect the fill hose from the compressor output to the rifle's fill port.
0START4,350 PSI
Step 02 / 05
Connect and start
With the bleed valve closed, power on the compressor. It will begin building pressure. PCP compressors typically output 1,500–4,500 PSI — expect noise and heat. Keep the unit ventilated and never leave it unattended.
0⚡ RUNNING4,350 PSI
Step 03 / 05
Monitor the fill
Watch the output gauge. Filling from low pressure can take 10–25 minutes depending on the compressor and reservoir size. Some compressors auto-shutoff at a set pressure — others require manual monitoring. Don't walk away.
0↑ FILLING…4,350 PSI
Step 04 / 05
Reach target pressure — stop
At your rifle's rated fill pressure, power off or allow the auto-shutoff to trigger. Do not add extra pressure. Some operators purge the moisture trap at this point — consult your compressor manual on purge frequency.
0TARGET ✓4,350 PSI
Step 05 / 05
Bleed, disconnect, cool down
Open the bleed valve to relieve line pressure. Disconnect from the fill port. Let the compressor run off-load for 1–2 minutes if it has a cooldown mode — this extends pump life significantly. Store the hose dry.
0CHARGED ✓4,350 PSI
Compressor pros: Fills from empty, no external cylinder needed, self-sufficient long term. Cons: Slow (10–25 min), generates heat and moisture, higher upfront cost, requires maintenance.
Hand Pump Fill Step 1 / 5
Step 01 / 05
Check pump and connections
Inspect the pump for damage. Ensure the bleed valve is closed and the pressure gauge is reading zero (some pumps need a moment). Most hand pumps connect via a Foster fitting directly to the rifle's fill port — attach it now.
0START4,350 PSI
Step 02 / 05
Begin pumping — slow and steady
Use your body weight over the pump handle, not just arm strength. The first 700–1,200 PSI (50–80 bar) come easily. As resistance builds, use a foot on the base and drive from your hips. Take it in sets — 20 strokes, rest, repeat. Rushing leads to overheating the pump seals.
0PUMPING…4,350 PSI
Step 03 / 05
Work through the resistance
Above 2,200 PSI (150 bar) the pump becomes genuinely hard work. Each stroke moves less air. Some PCP hand pumps have a 3-stage cylinder to manage this — the stages switch automatically. Keep your strokes full and deliberate. Watch the gauge between sets to track progress.
0↑ BUILDING4,350 PSI
Step 04 / 05
Approach target — slow final strokes
As you near your rifle's rated fill pressure, slow down and check the gauge frequently. It's easy to overshoot when you're working hard. Stop precisely at the rated pressure. Overfilling a PCP with a hand pump is a real risk — stay focused in the final 150 PSI (10 bar).
0NEARLY THERE4,350 PSI
Step 05 / 05
Open bleed, disconnect, rest
Open the pump's bleed valve — you'll hear a short hiss as line pressure is released. Disconnect the hose from the fill port. Leave the bleed cracked open when storing so the pump internals aren't held under pressure. The rifle is fully charged. Let yourself catch your breath.
0CHARGED ✓4,350 PSI
Hand pump pros: Portable, no power needed, lowest cost, fully self-sufficient in the field. Cons: Hard physical work, slow, pump seals need occasional maintenance, not practical for repeated top-ups.
Section 07

The Shot Cycle

A PCP fires in milliseconds. Here's what happens inside during that instant — step by step.

```
Shot Cycle Step 1 / 5
Step 01 / 05
Bolt closed — pellet loaded
The bolt is pushed forward, seating the pellet in the breech at the start of the barrel. The hammer is cocked — spring compressed, held by the sear. The valve is closed. Stored air in the reservoir is waiting.
Step 02 / 05
Trigger breaks — sear releases hammer
You squeeze the trigger. The sear disengages, releasing the hammer. The hammer spring drives the hammer forward at high speed toward the back of the valve.
Step 03 / 05
Hammer strikes the valve
The hammer impacts the valve stem, momentarily pushing it open against its spring tension. This creates a brief window — typically 5–15 milliseconds — during which high-pressure air flows from the reservoir through the valve and into the barrel.
Step 04 / 05
Air burst propels the pellet
The compressed air rushes behind the pellet, accelerating it down the barrel. The pellet engages the rifling — the spiral grooves cut into the barrel — which impart spin for stability in flight. By the time the pellet exits, it's spinning thousands of RPM.
Step 05 / 05
Valve closes — cycle complete
Valve spring pressure and reservoir back-pressure snap the valve shut once the hammer's energy is spent. Air flow stops. The reservoir retains the remaining pressure for the next shot. Cycle the bolt to eject the spent probe and load the next pellet.
Section 08

PCP Terms

The jargon decoded. Click any term to expand.

```
Unit of pressure Both measure pressure. PSI (pounds per square inch) is the standard in the US — it's what most American shooters use day-to-day. Bar is metric and appears on many imported rifles and fill equipment. 1 bar ≈ 14.5 PSI. So a 2,900 PSI fill is 200 bar; 3,335 PSI is 230 bar. Most PCP specs list both — check the reservoir stamp or manual for your rifle's rated max.
Internal component A precision valve that reduces and stabilises reservoir pressure to a fixed, lower setpoint before it reaches the firing valve. Regulated guns are generally more consistent shot-to-shot because the valve always sees the same working pressure regardless of how full the reservoir is. Most modern mid-to-high-end PCPs are regulated.
Air chamber A small secondary air chamber between the regulator and the valve. It acts as a buffer — storing a metered volume of air at regulated pressure, ready to be released in one consistent burst when the valve opens. Plenum volume affects how the shot feels and the efficiency of air use.
Internal component The weighted mass held under spring tension by the sear. When the trigger breaks, the hammer spring drives it forward to strike the valve stem, momentarily opening the valve and releasing air. Hammer weight, spring tension, and travel distance are key tuning variables — they control how long the valve stays open, and therefore how much air (and energy) each shot uses.
External connector The external connector point where your fill hose, dive bottle, or hand pump attaches to charge the reservoir. Most PCPs use a Foster-type quick-connect or a proprietary fitting. Some rifles have a built-in dust cap or check valve that prevents accidental depressurisation. Always ensure the fill port is compatible with your fill equipment before connecting.
Performance metric The sequence of shots from a full fill to empty, and how velocity changes across them. A regulated rifle's shot string is nearly flat (the "usable" portion). An unregulated rifle's shot string has a bell curve — velocity rises as pressure drops to the sweet spot, peaks, then falls. Knowing your rifle's shot string tells you exactly when accuracy starts degrading and it's time to refill.
Tool / accessory A tool or built-in feature that safely vents air from the reservoir when you need to service the rifle or transport it depressurised. Some rifles have a built-in degassing port; others require a purpose-made bleed adapter. Never attempt to disassemble a pressurised PCP — always fully depressurise first.
Muzzle device A device that attaches to the muzzle to reduce the report (sound) of each shot by slowing and cooling the escaping air. PCPs are well-suited to moderators because the air exits at relatively low pressure compared to firearms. The mechanical action of a PCP is whisper-quiet — with a good moderator, the loudest sound is often the pellet striking the target. Check your local laws before fitting one.
Gauge The pressure gauge built into or attached to the rifle's reservoir, showing current fill pressure. Lets you know at a glance how much air is left without guessing. Not all entry-level PCPs include one — without a manometer, you rely on the fill hose gauge. Having one on the rifle is a significant convenience during a session.
```
